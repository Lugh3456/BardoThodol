Stage 2 · I

Peaceful Deities, Days 1–2

← Overview Contents Days 3–4 →

On the first day, the Buddha Vairocana will appear, white in colour, seated upon a lion throne, holding a wheel, embracing his consort. From his heart a dazzling white light, the wisdom of the Dharmadhatu, will radiate toward you, so brilliant you cannot bear to look at it. At the same time, a dull white light from the god-realm will also shine. If you feel drawn to this softer light, and shrink from the brilliance of Vairocana, you will wander into the god-realm and circle again through birth and death. Therefore look upon the dazzling white light without fear; know it to be your own wisdom, the Dharmadhatu wisdom, and let yourself merge with it. On the second day, the Buddha Vajrasattva, deep blue, holding a vajra, will appear in similar manner, radiating a brilliant blue light, the mirror-like wisdom; alongside it a dull smoke-coloured light from the hell-realm will also shine. Do not be drawn to the smoke-coloured light. Recognize the blue radiance as the mirror-like wisdom, inseparable from your own awareness, and rest in it without fear or grasping.

Reading the Text

On the first day, the Buddha Vairocana will appear, white in colour... From his heart a dazzling white light, the wisdom of the Dharmadhatu, will radiate toward you, so brilliant you cannot bear to look at it.

Each day of the Chonyid Bardo presents a Buddha associated with one of the "five wisdoms" — transformations of the five basic afflictive emotions (the five poisons) into their corresponding wisdoms. Vairocana, associated with the centre of the mandala, represents the transformation of ignorance into the all-encompassing wisdom of the Dharmadhatu (the "basic space of phenomena"). The light is described as unbearably bright precisely because it is undiluted — ordinary perception filters and dims everything, and pure wisdom, unfiltered, can feel overwhelming rather than comforting.

At the same time, a dull white light from the god-realm will also shine. If you feel drawn to this softer light... you will wander into the god-realm and circle again through birth and death.

Alongside each Buddha's overwhelming wisdom-light, the text describes a much gentler, more bearable light corresponding to one of the six realms of rebirth — here, the god-realm. This pairing recurs throughout the Chonyid Bardo: a structural choice between an intense, "too much" experience of pure awareness, and a comfortable, familiar, but ultimately limiting alternative. The god-realm, though pleasant, is still within cyclic existence — its light is "softer" precisely because it is more familiar to an unawakened mind.

Therefore look upon the dazzling white light without fear; know it to be your own wisdom, the Dharmadhatu wisdom, and let yourself merge with it.

The instruction is consistent: the overwhelming light is not a separate, external Buddha radiating something at you — it is your own basic nature, displayed in its full intensity for perhaps the first time without the usual filters of ordinary perception. "Merging" with it is not submission to something foreign but a kind of recognition: ah, this is what I actually am, underneath all the filtering.

On the second day, the Buddha Vajrasattva, deep blue, holding a vajra, will appear in similar manner, radiating a brilliant blue light, the mirror-like wisdom; alongside it a dull smoke-coloured light from the hell-realm will also shine.

Vajrasattva (sometimes given as Akshobhya in other versions of the text) represents the transformation of anger or aversion into mirror-like wisdom — awareness that reflects everything clearly without distortion, the way a mirror reflects without being changed by what it shows. Its paired realm is the hell-realm, the destination most associated with anger and aversion in Buddhist cosmology. The pattern from day one repeats: an intense wisdom-light, and a dimmer, realm-specific alternative that corresponds to the very affliction being transformed.

Do not be drawn to the smoke-coloured light. Recognize the blue radiance as the mirror-like wisdom, inseparable from your own awareness, and rest in it without fear or grasping.

Smoke-coloured light leading to the hell realm is paired here with anger — a reminder that the realm one is drawn toward in the bardo is not arbitrary but reflects one's own habitual emotional patterns. The remedy offered is the same throughout: recognition of the wisdom-light as one's own nature, met without the two basic errors of fear (which would push it away) and grasping (which would turn it into an external object to chase).

Summary

The first two days establish the pattern that will repeat throughout the Chonyid Bardo: each day brings an overwhelmingly bright wisdom-light, paired with a dimmer, more comfortable light leading to one of the six realms. Vairocana's white light (Dharmadhatu wisdom, transforming ignorance) appears on day one alongside the god-realm's light; Vajrasattva's blue light (mirror-like wisdom, transforming anger) appears on day two alongside the hell-realm's light. In each case, the instruction is identical — recognize the intense light as one's own nature and rest in it, rather than retreating toward the familiar but limiting alternative.
