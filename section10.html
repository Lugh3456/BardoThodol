Stage 3 · II

Signs of Rebirth & Closing the Womb-Door

← The Judgment of Karma Contents Conclusion →

O nobly-born, if liberation has still not occurred, you will now see lights of six colours, dim and smoky, each leading toward one of the six realms of rebirth: white toward the gods, red toward the jealous gods, blue toward humans, green toward animals, yellow toward hungry ghosts, and smoke-coloured toward the hells. You will feel drawn toward one of these according to your karma, as if returning home after a long journey. At the same time, you may begin to perceive, as if from a great distance, the place and the parents of your next birth, and feel an impulse to enter. Before doing so, recall the instructions: whatever realm draws you, recognize that it too is empty of inherent existence, and if possible, generate compassion for all beings rather than aversion or desire toward the scene before you. If a favourable human rebirth seems likely, examine the prospective parents with care, and if possible, choose with the wish to be reborn where the Dharma can be practised. Above all, even at this final stage, do not forget: every appearance, including the womb itself, is a manifestation of mind, and recognizing this remains, until the very moment of conception, an opportunity for freedom.

Reading the Text

O nobly-born, if liberation has still not occurred, you will now see lights of six colours, dim and smoky, each leading toward one of the six realms of rebirth: white toward the gods, red toward the jealous gods, blue toward humans, green toward animals, yellow toward hungry ghosts, and smoke-coloured toward the hells.

These six lights echo, in a fainter register, the realm-lights that appeared alongside each Buddha's radiance during the Chonyid Bardo — but here they are not paired with anything brighter; they are simply present as options, or perhaps more accurately, as pulls. The deceased's task has shifted from choosing between an overwhelming wisdom-light and a comfortable realm-light, to navigating among the realm-lights themselves.

You will feel drawn toward one of these according to your karma, as if returning home after a long journey.

The phrase "as if returning home" is striking — it suggests that whichever realm a person is drawn toward will not feel strange or foreign, but familiar, even comfortable, regardless of whether it is, by any objective measure, a fortunate destination. This is consistent with the text's broader psychological insight: we are drawn toward what is familiar, and familiarity is shaped by habit, not by what is actually good for us.

At the same time, you may begin to perceive, as if from a great distance, the place and the parents of your next birth, and feel an impulse to enter.

This passage is often cited as one of the more concrete, almost narrative moments in the text — the deceased glimpsing scenes of the world they are about to enter, including, in some interpretations, the act that will lead to conception. The "great distance" framing suggests something like watching through a window rather than already being present, preserving, even here, a final gap in which choice remains possible.

Before doing so, recall the instructions: whatever realm draws you, recognize that it too is empty of inherent existence, and if possible, generate compassion for all beings rather than aversion or desire toward the scene before you.

Compassion appears here as a practical instruction, not merely an ethical ideal. Aversion and desire are presented as the very forces that propel one toward a less favourable rebirth (recoiling from one scene, grasping at another); compassion is offered as a stance that doesn't feed either reaction — it relates to the scene without either pushing it away or pulling it close.

If a favourable human rebirth seems likely, examine the prospective parents with care, and if possible, choose with the wish to be reborn where the Dharma can be practised.

This is one of the most practically-minded passages in the entire text — almost a piece of advice for choosing wisely among options, rather than an instruction toward liberation as such. It reflects the text's layered structure: liberation is the highest aim, but failing that, a fortunate rebirth — one that allows for spiritual practice — is presented as a worthwhile and achievable second goal, right up to the very last moment.

Above all, even at this final stage, do not forget: every appearance, including the womb itself, is a manifestation of mind, and recognizing this remains, until the very moment of conception, an opportunity for freedom.

The text closes this section by restating, for the final time, the single thread that has run through the entire journey: everything encountered, from the clear light at death to the scene of one's own conception, is a manifestation of mind. This is the last statement of the principle before the narrative portion of the text ends — a fitting bookend to the opening instructions of the Chikhai Bardo.

Summary

In the final stage of the Sidpa Bardo, the deceased encounters the dim lights of the six realms and feels drawn toward one according to their own karmic habits — a pull the text compares to the feeling of returning home. Even as the scene of the next rebirth comes into view, the text offers practical guidance: meeting the experience with compassion rather than aversion or desire, and, where a human rebirth is likely, choosing circumstances favourable to spiritual practice. The text's final reminder restates its central teaching one last time — everything, to the very last moment, is a manifestation of mind.
