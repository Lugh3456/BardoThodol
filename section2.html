Stage 1 · Chikhai Bardo

The Bardo of the Moment of Death

← Introduction Contents Chonyid Bardo →

O nobly-born, [name], the time has now come for you to seek the Path. Your breathing is about to cease. Your guru has set before you the aspect of the Ground Luminosity — now you are about to experience it in reality. In this first bardo, after all phenomena have dissolved, the luminosity of your true nature will appear before you, clear and empty, like a cloudless sky. At that moment, do not be afraid, do not shrink back. Recognize: this is your own true nature. Rest in it; do not chase after it, and do not push it away. If you recognize this luminosity as your own awareness, you will merge with the dharmakaya in that instant and attain liberation, never again to wander through the six realms. But because of the deep force of habitual karmic tendencies, most ordinary beings cannot remain in this first luminosity — it flashes and passes. A second, secondary luminosity will then arise, and there too lies a chance for recognition and liberation. If recognition still does not occur, consciousness will gradually enter the Chonyid Bardo, where visions arising from karmic tendencies will unfold one after another.

Reading the Text

O nobly-born, [name], the time has now come for you to seek the Path. Your breathing is about to cease. Your guru has set before you the aspect of the Ground Luminosity — now you are about to experience it in reality.

The text is written as a direct, second-person address, spoken into the ear of the dying or the recently dead. This is deliberate: it treats the dying person as still present, still capable of hearing and understanding, and frames what is coming not as a frightening unknown but as the fulfillment of teachings already received during life. "Ground Luminosity" refers to the fundamental, naturally luminous nature of mind, said to be present in everyone but normally obscured.

In this first bardo, after all phenomena have dissolved, the luminosity of your true nature will appear before you, clear and empty, like a cloudless sky.

The dissolution process described in Tibetan medical and tantric literature involves the gradual withdrawal of the elements and the coarser layers of consciousness. What remains, at the very end of this process, is a state of pure, unconditioned awareness — luminous (it knows) and empty (it has no fixed content or boundary). The "cloudless sky" image suggests something vast, open, and undisturbed, yet not blank — sky has the quality of clarity itself.

At that moment, do not be afraid, do not shrink back. Recognize: this is your own true nature. Rest in it; do not chase after it, and do not push it away.

This instruction is essentially a meditation instruction compressed into a single moment. The two errors it warns against — grasping and rejecting — are the same two errors meditation practice addresses throughout life: pursuing pleasant experiences and resisting unpleasant ones. At the moment of death, the text suggests, the habits of a lifetime either serve a person well or work against them; the instruction tries to interrupt the habitual reflex of fear at exactly the moment it would matter most.

If you recognize this luminosity as your own awareness, you will merge with the dharmakaya in that instant and attain liberation, never again to wander through the six realms.

Dharmakaya — the "truth body" — is, in this context, less a place to go than a way of being: the unmanufactured, ever-present ground of mind itself. To "merge" with it is not to travel anywhere but to stop mistaking the projections of mind for something separate from mind. The six realms (gods, demigods, humans, animals, hungry ghosts, and hell-beings) represent the full range of possible rebirths within cyclic existence — recognition here would mean exiting that cycle altogether.

But because of the deep force of habitual karmic tendencies, most ordinary beings cannot remain in this first luminosity — it flashes and passes. A second, secondary luminosity will then arise, and there too lies a chance for recognition and liberation.

This passage reflects the text's pastoral realism. It does not pretend that everyone will succeed at the hardest, briefest opportunity, and it does not abandon those who don't. The "secondary luminosity" is sometimes associated with a brief return of ordinary consciousness before it fully gives way to the bardo of becoming — a second doorway, slightly less demanding than the first, but still carrying the possibility of full recognition.

If recognition still does not occur, consciousness will gradually enter the Chonyid Bardo, where visions arising from karmic tendencies will unfold one after another.

This final line serves as a hinge to what follows. Having presented the cleanest, most direct opportunity for liberation, the text now turns toward a more elaborate — and for most people, more relevant — set of experiences: the visionary landscape of the Chonyid Bardo, where the qualities of mind appear externalized as deities, lights, and sounds, each carrying its own instruction for recognition.

Summary

The Chikhai Bardo is the briefest yet most direct opportunity for liberation in the entire Bardo Thodol: at the moment of death, the true face of mind — the Ground Luminosity — appears without any obscuration. To recognize it is liberation; to miss it is not the end of the journey, but the beginning of the next stage, in which visions arising from karma will unfold in sequence. The fundamental attitude conveyed here — neither pursuing nor rejecting, simply resting in awareness — is the thread that will run through every stage that follows.
