Modern Relevance of the Bardo Teachings

Reading the Text

The Bardo Thodol was composed as a guide for the literal process of dying, and that remains its primary purpose.

It's worth stating this plainly before turning to broader readings: the text's traditional context is funerary and pastoral. It was, and still is, recited at deathbeds and during the period of mourning, as a direct support for the dying and the deceased, and for the grieving who recite it on their behalf. Any wider application builds on, rather than replaces, this original use.

But across the centuries, and especially since its introduction to Western readers, it has also been read in another way: as a map of the mind under pressure, applicable not only at the moment of death but in any situation where ordinary stability gives way and powerful experiences — pleasant or terrifying — seem to overwhelm us from outside.

This broader reading became especially prominent through the work of later interpreters — both Tibetan teachers writing for new audiences, and Western psychologists and writers who found in the text a vivid vocabulary for inner experience. The shift is from "this will happen to you once, after death" to "something structurally similar happens to you regularly, in smaller forms, throughout life."

Sleep and dreaming, intense emotion, moments of crisis, and meditative experience have all been compared to bardo states by later teachers.

The comparison to dreaming is especially old and well-developed within the Tibetan tradition itself — dream yoga and bardo practice are closely linked, on the premise that learning to recognize the dream-like, mind-made quality of experience during sleep builds exactly the capacity needed during the after-death bardos. Intense emotion and crisis extend this further: moments when our usual sense of a stable, separate self feels shaken.

In each case, the text's central instruction translates without much difficulty: what arises, however vivid, is not separate from the mind experiencing it; it need not be chased, and it need not be feared.

This is the instruction, stated one final time, stripped of its bardo-specific imagery. Whether the "vivid arising" is a wrathful deity, a nightmare, a wave of grief, or a flash of overwhelming joy, the same two-part guidance applies: don't grasp at it, and don't run from it. The text's elaborate visionary content can be understood as a series of worked examples for a principle that is, at root, simple to state and difficult to live.

Read this way, the Bardo Thodol is less a manual for a single future event than a training in a way of meeting experience that can be practised, in small ways, throughout life — so that when the larger transitions come, whatever they may be, they are met with the same steadiness.

This closing thought reframes the entire text retrospectively. Everything described across the previous sections — the lights, the deities, the judgment, the realms — can be approached not only as a description of what happens after death, but as an invitation to notice how the mind already behaves: how it brightens, darkens, frightens, and consoles itself, moment to moment, long before any final transition arrives.

Summary

This closing section steps back from the narrative of the bardo journey to consider why the Bardo Thodol has found such a wide readership beyond its original funerary context. Its core instruction — recognize what arises as a manifestation of mind, neither chasing nor fearing it — applies, by extension, to dreams, strong emotions, and moments of crisis throughout life. Read this way, the text offers not only a guide for one's final transition, but a way of practising, in everyday experience, for whatever transitions life brings.
