Stage 2 · V

The Wrathful Deities

← Day 7 Contents The Judgment of Karma →

O nobly-born, on the eighth day and after, the Great Glorious Buddha-Heruka and the wrathful deities of the five families will appear — bodies dark red or blackish-brown, many-headed, many-armed, garlanded with skulls, standing amid raging fire, with eyes like staring lightning and roars like a thousand thunders. These are the same five Buddhas you have already seen, now appearing in wrathful form. Do not be afraid, and do not flee. Know that these terrifying forms are your own thoughts, projected outward, taking on a fierce appearance — they have no power to harm you, for there is nothing outside your own mind that could harm you. As the days continue, female wrathful deities, and finally the eight Kerima and eight Htamenma and other protective and wrathful forms, will likewise appear, equally vast and equally terrifying. In every case the same instruction applies: recognize these as the wrathful aspect of your own awareness, the same energy that appeared peacefully before, now wearing a different face. Fear is the only obstacle; there is nothing else to overcome.

Reading the Text

O nobly-born, on the eighth day and after, the Great Glorious Buddha-Heruka and the wrathful deities of the five families will appear — bodies dark red or blackish-brown, many-headed, many-armed, garlanded with skulls, standing amid raging fire, with eyes like staring lightning and roars like a thousand thunders.

The wrathful deities (Herukas) form the second great cycle of the Chonyid Bardo. Their iconography — multiple heads and arms, garlands of skulls, surrounding flames — is drawn from a long tradition of wrathful tantric imagery, where ferocity represents not malevolence but the intensity of compassion and wisdom directed at destroying obstacles, including the obstacle of one's own clinging and fear.

These are the same five Buddhas you have already seen, now appearing in wrathful form.

This identification is crucial and easy to miss on a first reading: the text is not introducing a new set of beings with a different relationship to the deceased. It is saying that the same five figures encountered peacefully on days one through five now appear wrathfully. The change is in the deceased's relationship to the material — what was approachable now appears overwhelming — not in the material itself.

Do not be afraid, and do not flee. Know that these terrifying forms are your own thoughts, projected outward, taking on a fierce appearance — they have no power to harm you, for there is nothing outside your own mind that could harm you.

This is perhaps the most psychologically direct line in the whole text. It locates the source of the terror entirely within the perceiver, and draws the logical consequence explicitly: if the frightening forms are projections of one's own mind, and there is nothing outside the mind in this state, then there is, by definition, no external agent that could cause harm. The danger, such as it is, lies entirely in the reaction of fear itself — which, the text suggests elsewhere, is what propels a being toward an unfavourable rebirth.

As the days continue, female wrathful deities, and finally the eight Kerima and eight Htamenma and other protective and wrathful forms, will likewise appear, equally vast and equally terrifying.

The text describes an extended sequence of further wrathful and protective figures beyond the principal Herukas — a proliferation that can feel, on first encounter, endless. Rather than treating each new figure as requiring a separate analysis, the text deliberately keeps repeating the same single instruction, almost as a refrain, trusting that the instruction itself — not memorizing who's who — is what matters.

In every case the same instruction applies: recognize these as the wrathful aspect of your own awareness, the same energy that appeared peacefully before, now wearing a different face. Fear is the only obstacle; there is nothing else to overcome.

By the end of this section, the text has reduced an enormous, elaborate cast of figures to a single point: peaceful and wrathful are two faces of one thing, and the only real variable is the viewer's own fear. This simplicity is the payoff of everything built up across the previous days — all the detail about colours, implements, and paired realms ultimately serves this one, repeatedly restated recognition.

Summary

The wrathful deities are presented not as a new threat but as the same five Buddhas already encountered, now appearing in their fierce aspect, followed by an extended retinue of wrathful and protective forms. The text's central claim is uncompromising: nothing in this display can cause harm, because nothing here is external to the mind experiencing it. The only real danger is fear itself — the same instruction that has run through every stage so far, now tested against its most intense material.
