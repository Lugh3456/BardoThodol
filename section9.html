Stage 3 · I

Sidpa Bardo — The Judgment of Karma

← The Wrathful Deities Contents Signs of Rebirth →

O nobly-born, if you have not recognized any of the visions described so far, you will now enter the Sidpa Bardo, the bardo of seeking rebirth. Here your former clarity will fade, and habitual tendencies will reassert themselves; you will once again feel as though you have a body, though it is still only a mental body, formed of your own karmic propensities. You will appear before the Lord of Death, Shinje, and see reflected the Mirror of Karma, in which every action of your life, good and bad, is shown without concealment. A White Spirit and a Black Spirit, representing your own virtuous and unvirtuous tendencies, will count out white and black pebbles — one for each deed. Do not be afraid, and do not attempt to lie, for nothing can be hidden. Yet know also that this entire judgment, the judge, the mirror, the pebbles, and the verdict, is itself a projection of your own mind — there is, in truth, no one judging you but yourself. If at this point you can recognize even this — that the Lord of Death himself has no inherent existence — you may still attain liberation.

Reading the Text

O nobly-born, if you have not recognized any of the visions described so far, you will now enter the Sidpa Bardo, the bardo of seeking rebirth. Here your former clarity will fade, and habitual tendencies will reassert themselves; you will once again feel as though you have a body, though it is still only a mental body, formed of your own karmic propensities.

The Sidpa Bardo marks a third major shift in the text's tone. The Chonyid Bardo's visions, however intense, were explicitly framed as displays of awareness in something close to its natural state; here, that clarity recedes, and the deceased's own habitual patterns — accumulated over a lifetime (and many lifetimes) — begin to dominate experience again. The mental body "formed of karmic propensities" suggests something more conditioned, more like an ordinary personality, than the luminous mental body of the Chonyid Bardo.

You will appear before the Lord of Death, Shinje, and see reflected the Mirror of Karma, in which every action of your life, good and bad, is shown without concealment.

The figure of Shinje (a wrathful judge of the dead, related to Yama in broader Buddhist and Hindu cosmology) and the Mirror of Karma are vivid, almost theatrical images — a courtroom scene complete with judge, evidence, and tally. For many readers, this is the most immediately recognizable section of the text, resonating with judgment imagery found across many religious traditions.

A White Spirit and a Black Spirit, representing your own virtuous and unvirtuous tendencies, will count out white and black pebbles — one for each deed. Do not be afraid, and do not attempt to lie, for nothing can be hidden.

The white and black pebbles — a simple, almost folk-tale image — represent an exact accounting, with no possibility of distortion or concealment, because (as the text immediately goes on to clarify) the "accountants" are aspects of the deceased's own mind. There is no point in trying to deceive an examiner who is, in the end, oneself.

Yet know also that this entire judgment, the judge, the mirror, the pebbles, and the verdict, is itself a projection of your own mind — there is, in truth, no one judging you but yourself.

This single sentence reframes the entire scene that precedes it. Having presented one of the most vivid and culturally familiar images in the text — a courtroom judgment — the text immediately undercuts its apparent externality. This is consistent with everything that has come before: every figure encountered in the bardo, however official or authoritative it appears, is ultimately the deceased's own mind in a particular configuration.

If at this point you can recognize even this — that the Lord of Death himself has no inherent existence — you may still attain liberation.

Even at this late stage, deep into the Sidpa Bardo, the text holds the door to liberation open. The opportunity here is more abstract than earlier ones — not a luminous Buddha to merge with, but a conceptual recognition about the nature of an apparently fearsome authority figure — yet the text insists it carries the same weight. This persistence of opportunity, right up until rebirth itself, is one of the text's most striking and, for many readers, most consoling features.

Summary

The Sidpa Bardo opens with the fading of the clarity that characterized the Chonyid Bardo, and the reassertion of habitual karmic tendencies. Its central image — judgment before the Lord of Death, with a mirror reflecting every deed and spirits tallying good and bad actions — is one of the most vivid in the text, and also one the text immediately reframes: judge, mirror, and verdict are all projections of the deceased's own mind. Even here, recognizing the empty nature of this apparently authoritative figure remains a path to liberation.
